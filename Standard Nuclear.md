TRISO was a little bit of an art, there was American, German, Japanese TRISO, and people didn't understand why until the early 2000s when the DoE created the [[Advanced Gas Reactor Program]], which took it from an art to a science and unlocked the potential of TRISO.

Why TRISO? Billions of discrete pressure vessels made out of silicon carbine, really strong, doesn't melt... The issue is that you don't want to release radioactivity, and if you can entrap radioactivity at the core, at the fuel itself, then you don't have a radiological problem.

You put these TRISO poppyseeds into a form/shape so that you aren't just pouring them into the reactor. Typically you're using some type of carbonaceous/graphite matrix, or silicon carbide.
- You can put them into a tennis-ball-like sphere, called a ==Pebble==
- You can put them into a cylinder, called a ==Compact==
- You can have fancier shapes too.
This is just the fuel form so it's not loose particles.

In the 70s/80s, we built a lot of fuel in the US, the Germans had [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]]s, the UK had them, etc.

==The only TRISO-based [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]] reactors that are operating today are in China==
- They literally bought the blueprints and are making them.

TRISO is probably the worst form of nuclear form to try to do anything with, because of the coatings. Trying to break that down is no easy feat. It's such small amounts, and you'd have to enrich it further... it's a processed product. One of the amazing things that Kurt has done... wasn't just that they could make TRISO, or make it in big batches... they'd optimized so that the yield of the process was very high. If you unpack the implications of something like that, you don't just take Uranium that doesn't meet the spec and toss it out, it's a hazardous material... the ability to take those particles after they're made and break them down into their parts to reprocess it is something that they (Kurt, Standard Nuclear) did that drives down price and increases efficiency.

- Kurt: Dawn of Nuclear Era, 40s/50s, the [[Atoms for Peace]]... "Let's use this awesome source of energy that will be too cheap to meter."  Engineers come in, and say there's an awesome amount of power. Multiple Gigawatts of energy in a volume less than this room, for instance. Enough to power millions of homes. The power density per unit volume... when you turn the reactor off, you still generate a lot of heat. 6%, 5% of this is still a lot of power. So they said "Let's scale these things to make them very big." and then say "Hey, man, what's the worst thing that can happen?" They listed them... and said "These are going to be our bounding accidents; we've designed a reactor so that if these extreme events occur, it can be safe." That's called an ==Active Safety System,== where something kicks in to control your system. What happens if a long-tale event happens, they call that a "Beyond Design Basis" accident, and just don't design for that. So if you look at Fukushima. with a 9.X scale earthquake. You have an awesome amount of power, a 1960s design that isn't designed for a reactor on the ring of fire.
- Big reactors with huge power density, imagine if you put a dragon in a cage and say "Hey, if that gets out, that's bad!" So they put another cage around it, and call that ==defense in depth==, but eventually it's going to get out.
- The new reactors have much lower power density, it's like a bunny running on a wheel, they've accounted for the long tail, and are relying on natural phenomenon, it's ==Passive Safety System====.
