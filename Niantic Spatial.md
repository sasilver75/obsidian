
==Scaniverse== product: Users can scan a space with their phones and quickly create a [[Gaussian Splatting|3DGS]] gaussian splatting of it, with no need for things like Matterport.


> A challenge we think about a lot is how to combine different data modalities. We work a lot with different data here, whether t' [[OpenStreetMap|OSM]] or [[United States Geological Survey|USGS]] data, and we're focused on being able ti incorporated different types of data into that... in a way that you can traverse, if you're travelling through the world where you move from a space where you're localizing spatial data using a broad, coarse dataset like OSM... to, inside of a private business or home, utilizing that data in a seamless fashion. Thats' a tough problem I'm interested in.
> - Tory Smith

> Q: Those of us in the industry didn't even think about mobile-generated 3D maps from [[Gaussian Splatting|3DGS]] even being maps! How do you think of it?
> A: The founders go back to Keyhole, Google Earth, etc. Specifically for a game like Pokemon Go, there was an opt-in feature where you could take a vide of a PokeStop, a real-world, publically accessible location. You could upload that video, and we eventually used those videos to build very accurate models of those real-world locations, both to prove that you actually visited, and for allowing you to anchor specific pieces of content for other people to discover at those locations. In doing that, we invested a lot in three-dimensional construction, specifically building a Gaussian Splat... a vivid model that's similar to a mesh but higher fidelity, where you can see opacity and reflections and things like that. In terms of what we're doin now, we want to take that same capability, but use other sources of data (open data, messy data, data form many types of sensors). We're trying to get this to work with many types of data, including data that customers might bring themselves, or maybe an entity has their own set of data... the recurring theme is trying to do more with less.
>A lot of people are interested in things like change detection... not minute to minute change... one of the challenges with traditional survey data is that it's quite expensive, so you can't get data every month or year... and being able to work with crowdsourced data is very useful if you want models that update every 15 minutes, or something like that.

> Q: Wow, tech advances on phones have allowed you to do a lot... do you see it more as sensor evolution or processing evolution?
> A: I'm going to say.... neither ! From Tory's perspective, it's more about use cases... following the advent of... we take it for granted now that you can always get traffic information to get an ETA... and the reason why this works is that there's a very long process of building incredible value first to get lots of usage, before you can then start to.. benefit from the exhaust and side-effects from that. Google maps for intsance, great product with tons of mobile app usage, and then by merit of creating so much value with a free product (same with Wayze), it creates a self-reinforcing effect, and then you have so much quantity of data that you can start to get a lot of benefits, even though the data is quite close on a probe by probe level. This is like what MobilEye has done. One of the holy grails of mapping is building the notion of a flywheel where the deployed devices are updating the map... but before you can entertain that concept, you have to create a whole bunch of value FIRST. It's kind of like trying ot start a perpetual motion machine; it's quite challenging, you have to find a very valuable problem to solve first, for millions, if not for hundreds of thousands of users. This requires being very clever or very rich.
