  - Solder

Joining metals by melting a low-temperature filler metal called a ==solder== that bonds to the base metals without melting them. When heated, it flows into the joint by wetting the surfaces, and often by capillary action. 
- Typically used for electrical connection in electronics, e.g. circuit boards, or wire connections.
- Usually requires a ==[[Flux]]== (a chemical cleaning agent to help solder bond properly) to clean oxides and help the solder flow. Without Flux, solder often beads up, refuses to stick, or forms a weak, dull, reliable connection.
	- Rosin Flux is the most common type used in electronics.

c.f. [[Brazing]] and [[Welding]]
- Both of these processes join metals by melting a filler metal into the joint without melting the base metals.
- The main difference is *temperature*
	- Soldering: Filler metal melts below ~450C/842F
		- Primary purpose is electrical connection for electronics, light joining, sealing.
	- Brazing: Filler metal melts above ~450C/842F, but still below the melting point of the base metals.
		- Stronger mechanical joint, sealing, dissimilar-metal joining. Used when you need more strength than soldering, but less heat than [[Welding]] (which melts the two base metals).
