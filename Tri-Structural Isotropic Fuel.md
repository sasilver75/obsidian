A highly robust, accident-tolerant micro-particle nuclear fuel designed to never melt, even under extreme reactor conditions.

Each particle consists of a uranium, carbon, and oxygen kernel coated in carbon and ceramic layers, enabling it to act as its own containment system.

![[Pasted image 20260507181143.png]]


[[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]]-based reactors have historically faced LOW [[Capacity Factor (Energy)|Capacity Factor]]s (14-40%) due to significant maintenance challenges, engineering complexities, and technical limitations requiring more frequent shutdowns compared to conventional fuel.

This is instead of the typical fuel, which is a Uranium-Dioxide pellet.

> I want to put the reactors anywhere in the world, and so I want them to be build with a really high degree of safety with respect to release of anything radioactive. We have about 120,000,000 for a 120kg of them, in a core that's only 4 feet in diameter... You can go to 1600C before there's an appreciable failure rate of the ceramic-coated fuel, so you can't melt down the fuel, which removes one of the big risks common to reactors that use the Uranium Dioxide pellets and Zirconium 2, which is the standard for [[Light Water Reactor]] technology. We also have a helium system... helium can't become radioactive either; it can't actually absorb a neutron, which is what makes something radioactive. If the unit ever leaks (which it could if it were knocked over by an environmental disaster, etc)... if gas comes out, that gas isn't dangerous. Having a more expensive form of fuel, a more expensive coolant (vs. water)... these two things allow for you to have no leak of radioactive liquid ever to the environment.

Steps:
1. Package Uranium into sphericalk ernels using the sol-gel process.
2. uranium oxie is dissilved in nitric acide to make a broth solution
3. This is cooled and dispersed in droplets into an oil bath.
4. Droplets undergo reactions that make them soften into gel spheres, a few mm in diameter
5. The spheres gently flow to collectors to be cleaned, rinsed, and drained. 2KG of uranium fuel kernels per hour, equivalent energy to 600,000 liters of diesel per hour ,or 24 truck tanks. 
6. Kernels are prepared for a high temperature furnace... and coated with a series of engineered ceramics layers using chemical vapor deposition. 
7. The thickness, porosity, grain size, and texture are strictly controlled to achieve desired performance.
8. Gases are flowed to to create a fluidized beds, which mixes and suspends the particles in the chamber.
9. The particles are kept airborne, and layers can be chemically deposited evenly and consistency.
10. Particles can then be sorted and selected to make sure we only deliver high-quality spherical particles of a specific diameter.
11. Size separation is achieved using roller pins or screens.
12. Misshapen or non-spherical particles are sorted using an inclined or vibratory plane.
13. Each lot of TRISO particles and FCM fuel particles are randomly sampled and inspected using optical microscopy to measure the thickness of TRSISO layers, along with other techniques. Images are analyzed using automated image analysis techniques.
14. The TRISO particles are ready to be embedded in asolit pellet. We embed them in a silicon matrix to enhance fission product retention and stability of the fuel form.
15. Binder jet powder printers form the vessels for the TRISO particles. Additively manufacturing the shells allows for complex geometries and rapid prototyping without retooling.
16. Powder bed is baked in an oven to complete the cure... after a few hours, the parts can be depowdered in a dedicated hood.
17. The shells, lso called Gree Bodies, can now be filled with TRISO particles (and silicon carbide powder) into the shells, using vibration to get maximal packing.
18. We can now use chemical vapor infiltration to fill in the gaps and achieve a sealed layer on teh outside of the pellet.
19. Parts are loaded on a multi-level retort. Gas is flowed at high temp to deposit reaction products otnto the ceramic surfaces. After several days, the deposition is complete, and the pellets are removed for final inspection.
20. Industry standard TRISO fuel is encased with fully dense silicon carbide matrix, a combination which provides an extremely rugged and stable fuel with extraordinary high temperature stability.
