Q: How do you prevent thermal runaway?
A: Let me explain why nuclear reactors blow up
- Uranium atoms, if one splits, it produces two neutrons (?), and one of the neutrons will hopefully hit another Uranium atom, which causes it to split, and it's exponential growth. 
- This is actually not true; when the Neutron leaves the first Uranium atom, it's actually going too fast to cause Fission. You have to slow it down before it contacts another Uranium atom before it contacts another Uranium atom in order to cause another fission event. 
- To slow it down, we bump it into a bunch of smaller atoms first, which slows it down, and then you can contact another Uranium. This is called a [[Moderator]], which is a substance that sits between the nuclear fuel and slows the Neutrons down so that they can cause more fission. The traditional moderator is Nuclear Reactor is water. The function of the water is two-fold:
	- To remove the heat, which is useful
	- To moderate the Neutrons, because hydrogen is an effective Neutron moderator
- But there's a safety issue there: It wants to be steam when it gets hot (as reactors do). Most [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]]s work above the boiling point of water, and so they're hyper-pressurized to maintain water as a liquid. There's a default problem built into these designs:
	- The water needs to be hot so that it's efficient
	- The water really wants to be steam
	- When it becomes steam accidentally, there's a 1500x pressure spike (it's not linear)
	- That's the basic safety issue with LWR
- We circumvent that entirely, using a design that exists.
- We use carbon, we use Graphite as the moderator.
	- Graphite starts as a solid in our reactor, and stays a solid. It never changes phase.
	- KEY SAFETY POINT: It turns out that moderators are temperature dependent; as they heat up, they become less and less effective as moderators. [[Thermal Coefficient of Reactivity]]. If your reactor gets hotter and hotter, if you moderator is designed correctly, it will just shut down the reaction, because it stops slowing down those neutrons. For Carbon/Graphite, that's about 1000C, where the moderation effect ceases, and it melts at 2500C. So the melting point is way above the point at which is stops moderating neutrons. It's meltdown proof, and is the principle of [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]] designs for a long time.
- I think it's... the safest type of reactor, and all reactors should be built this way for a while.
- 3 months ago china just did this: They had an operating reactor, running it at temp, and decided to just turn the power off to the facility, and see what happened: The temperature rose to 1050, the moderator failed to moderate the neutrons, and ti shut down. It's the safest type of reactor, and all reactors should be built this was for a while.
