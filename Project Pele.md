He started PELE in the Fall of 2018, and when he started there were significant anti-nuclear forces in the pentagon and government who just didn't like the word "Nuclear." This isn't gone, but it has diminished significantly.

The [[ADVANCE Act]], a bill to reform the [[Nuclear Regulatory Commission|NRC]], just passed 88-2 bipartisan.

In 2018, Congress put some language in a Defense Authorization bill asking for a plan to make Nuclear power happen, recognizing that energy has gotten more important and that nuclear has advanced a long way from the 60s. 
- The undersecretary of defense for research and engineering recognized that reports aren' that useful...
- So I was handed this project, with a whitepaper of "here's what a nuclear reactor might look like," and we ended up developing that further.

It basically looks like 4 shipping containers:
- Reactor
- Intubated heat exchanger
- Power conversion turbine
- A distance way, a fourth box that's the operating module.

Officially, we say the reactor will put out 1-5MW of power, but more coser to 1.

The program started having funding in 2020... design process kicked off right before COVID, and have been proceeding at full pace since then.

Our winning vendor is [[BWXT]]. 

We're currently (Dec 2024) figuring out last parts of the design, most of the design is locked in, and for those parts that are locked in, we've begun ordering the hardware. We've made most of the new fuel, etc. It's no longer a paper reactor.

In 2025...we've begun to assemble the reactor. We will also finish making the fuel, and we assume it will be completed and finished by the middle of the year... and will lay the first concrete at the site in Idaho.

Predecessor to Pele is the [[Army Nuclear Power Program]]... Camp Century, buried under an ice sheet in Greenland, another in Antarctica, another in Wyoming... and this program fizzled. The Army found that in the end, the premium betwteen the cost/operational headache/etc... was not worth it, and diesel generators had also gotten a lot better. Army decided that it's not worth the ∆ in cost.

So what's reviving interest?

A: There's an old saying of amateurs study tactics, and professionals study logistics. As a kid, I knew the names of the generals from civil war, WW2, etc... and when you get older, you start to realize that in fact none of that stuff really matters: What decides modern warfare between near peers is logistics more than anything else. The battles took place in WW2 where the logistical hardpoints were, Japan started the war because of Japan's inability to access oil, and warfare is all about targeting eachotehrs' energy and logistical resources. We won WW2 not because we had more people or better equipment, but because we completely won the logistics battle, getting the oil, steel, and everything else we needed, and our adversaries could not. This stayed as a theme into the 40s and 50s. In WW2 they had a bunch of power ships that could deliver power to shore (e.g. powering what we did in northern Normandy at D-Day). Those ships were still around so that when the Korean war happened, when we were pushed down to the bottom of Korea and lost access to the Korean electric grid, we had power ships offshore delivering power to the force! This led to the Army Nuclear Program, which was stood up at the same time as the Navy nuclear program. The Army had a nuclear reactor on a boat! It's most notably thing is that it powered the Panama canal for a few years. As we went into Vietnam, logistics became not as big of a problem. We didn't have to worry about Vietnamese subs sinking our oil hundreds of miles offshore. We were in a budget crunch, and we asked what the cheapest way to deliver power was, and the answer was fossil fuels.

Against the enemies we've had for the last few decades, logistics has stopped being challenged. In the Gulf War, tanks made their famous left hook to try to keep the Iraqi republican guard stuck in Kuwait so we could destroy them piecemeal, and those tanks had to move and not stop, and those big Abrams tanks used 2 gal/mi. We put fuel depots literally ahead of the tanks so that they could catch up to the fuel depot, refuel, and immediately go on their way, using the 101st airborne to guard. WE could do that because we had no respect to the Iraqi Airforce or Missile program to hit these things.

As we start to think about near-peer adversaries, we have to think a lot more seriously about our ability to move things around. We don't just need to fear our oil tankers being taken out, but our electric grid in the United States as well. We know that China is on our electric grid, listening to phones, etc. Cyber attacks on the grid will happen when a great power conflict happens.

Right now in the DoD, all of our reliable power needs are delivered by fossil fuel, almost exclusively diesel generator sets, which is fine, but inefficient and wasteful and assumes that you'll have a healthy supply of fossil fuels going forward. In modern warfare, you can't do anything without power... We are long past a world of people with rifles shooting eachother. This has gotten the defense science board to think a lot more seriously... and led Congress to say "Yes, absolutely, let's find an alternative!" And this is why Project Pele has gotten the funding that it has!

If we look at the islands that the US has west of Hawaii, all of them receive their energy (fossil fuels) from the west, from the middle east, through the malacca strait, and to those islands on foreign-flagged ships. Our ability to get fossil fuels to those islands will be an immense problem.

Q: I want to shift gears to get into the technical details... why don't we drop into jumping into exactly what thsi reactor is you're building. Let's nerd out as much as you can on the technical specifications.
A: I'm somewhat limited on what I can talk about with the design, but yes, it's a [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]]-based reactor, an AGR variant of TRISO... It's 19.75% enriched, [[High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium|HALEU]] fuel. It's a [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]], and it's an open [[Brayton Cycle]] (the secondary is air).... unfortunately I'm awfully limited about what I can say about the system, but if you visualize a HTGR, those are reasonably accurate.

Q: Rumor on the street is that TRISO is both an opportunity and a constraint; it's safe, has its own containment system built in, leads you to having a HTGR system.
A: I did require that companies had to design around the fuel, TRISO, which is very expensive.


An example of how we're able to leverage [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]] to reduce costs... is earthquake resistance.
There's a seismic scale, 1-5; Nuclear reactors typically have to be resistant to 5.
To do this requires an incredibly expensive civil engineer process. The argument we made to regulators is that We don't need to be earthquake proof; if the structure collapses on the reactor, that's okay! 
The give and take of TRISO; you have to be smart about which reactors use TRISO: ==You have to be smart about which sort of reactors use TRISO, because it's more expensive to make the fuel; you need to make sure that you're saving that money somewhere else, so it's worth it.==



Q: Some people ask why aren't you doing something like a scaled down naval nuclear reactor
A: The literal naval reactor design... those designs are very expensive. The navy has so many incredible performance requirements for their reactors that our reactors are not going to need; you don't want to have to pay for that performance. The naval reactors are the most incredible reactors in the world, but you pay for that; handling off-nominal conditions (tilt), being able to go from off to on really quick, etc. We don't need that and don't want to pay for it.
For [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]], you wouldn't put it in an [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]], because one of the big advantages of TRISO is that it can handle very very high temperatures. Even this basic form of TRISO has been tested for 300 hours at 1800 degrees celsius with no meaningful amount of breakage, which is extraordinarily high. If you look at more advanced forms of TRISO that people are working on, they can go higher, to 2400 to 2800C. Operating at higher temperatures lets you have a more efficient reactor, as well as process heat; if we want to decarbonize industrial processes that require that high heat.

But we didn't actually CHOOSE a [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]]. We were open to other concepts, although a molten salt reactor would be hard from a transportability perspective, because it freezes at room temperature.
Most of the advanced reactor companies...almost all of them want to use [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]], for safety benefits, regulatory benefits, and the ability to operate at high temperature.


Q: Trying to fit ... into a really small package is interesting. Getting this thing into four shipping containers.
A: 
- We rejected supercritical Co2 because we though the [[Technology Readiness Level]] (TRL) was too low.
- Getting everything into a small box is difficult; shielding is a big part of it; if you look at it, the core is a very small part of the box, it's mostly shielding.
- Things like the intubated heat exchanger, squeezing that into a box, is also very difficult to do.

You have these very high temperatures, but have room temperature also awfully close together, which puts huge amount of thermal strain on all of these materials. A lot of the models and codes that are approved for reactors, these ASME codes, they break down at these temperatures ranges... so we discovered a typo in the ASME code (A model off by a factor of two; until you put these thermal stressors on real materials, you don't realize it)... We found that, actually, these codes give you the entirely wrong answer! This has been our biggest challenge, is dealing with thermal stressors. So when I meet with these microreactor companies, I ask "What are you doing with thermal stresses? It's a really difficult challenge." You have to get from very high temperature to room temperature in short distance, which impacts your design in a lot of ways.

Q: So this is going to be built by [[BWXT]] out in Virginia, and then moved to [[Idaho National Laboratory|INL]]... talk about that capcity you're trying to build... Moving a reactor that's been on... how do you work around some of that. This takes into acccount the Turran Test, which is that the seriousness of a microreactor developer can be judged by the degree to which they show their shielding in their schematics... talk about shielding!
A: 
- As people know, once you turn off a reactor, you stop creating neutrons immediately, so what kills you is you secondary radiation; things in your system that get irradiated in the reactor and radiate back at you.
- Certain materials that might be hot for... initially, but might have a 12 hour half life and you can just wait em out.
- Other ones, like Cobalt.. if you have Cobalt, Cobalt's half life is a couple years... So if you get Cobalt hot, you have a real problem moving anything.
- So designing a tiny reactor, you have to knock down your neutrons as quickly as possible, keeping them as contained as possible to minimize how much you do (?) secondary radiation.
- The concret structurei sj ust for knocking down gammas, which is critical when thinking about using it in the real world. If the concrete structure is knocking down neutrons, then that concrete is going to be irradiated, and it's difficult to get rid of. Concrete structure nocking down gammas, on hte other hand, doesn't cause [[Secondary Radiation]].
- Among the ways that we'll knock down neutrons, which is a repeat of the way the army did it bakc in the dayr, is to put water around the reactor.
	- Empty water containers, fill with distilled water when you get to the site.
	- Keep in mind that impurities in water... you have to monitor... It's not trivial, but the most obvious way to do it and keep weight down.
- ==Nuclear shielding is a physics problem, there's no magical solution to [[High-Z]], it's very helpful. Finding stuff locally that you can use for that shielding is critical if you want to be able to move that stuff locally.==

Q: A question that comes up with shielding and microreactors... it sounds like you're putting the power conversion system in another area, which should be rad-safe on its own secondary loop. The heat exchanger has radioactive stuff hitting it, but the helium isn't going to carry a lot... In terms of an operating reactor, [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]]s have been pretty buggy; capacity factors at [[Fort Saint Vrain Nuclear Power Plant]] famously were at 16%, [[Peach Bottom Nuclear Atomic Power Station]] a little bit better.
 But generally, talking to some Germans who ran HTGRs, it was white knuckling, sweating bullets keeping that reactor going, a lot of trouble shooting, If you have to go in and tweak that reactor, torque wrenches, whatever maintaining a reactor looks like... how does that interplay with the shielding challenge, because everything is so much tighter.
A: When I started on Pele, we wanted to see... what designers were out there for microreactors. Looking at cartoons on the internet, they usually put he whole system in one box, and the Defense Science Board report that got his all started was a 40 foot container that had all these systems in it. One of the first choices on Pele was to get rid of that. If you have the whole reactor system in one box, you'll have two really big problems:
1. You reactor putting out a ton of Neutrons will beat on... your turbine blades, for isntance... and will break and become readioactive fast. 
2. If you wnat ot do maintenance, how do you get into that thing? You'll have to dress up liek an extra from Chernobyl. The maintenance is typically not on the reactor part, it's on things that spin and rub, bearings and gears and things like that.

==So we decided very early on that we would want to put things in separate 20 foot boxes instead of a 40 foot box==. This allows us to put a significant amount of shielding between the reactor and the power conversion model, and also make the maintenance a little easier.

Still be a pain though, we have to be cautious:
- On a first of a kind, you can model what you think will happen, but you might be off.
- We have to be wary: There might be a little bit of dust inside that concrete module, what happens if they get activated? You might breathe it in! So we'll have to be very cautious... but that's one of the benefits of doing this at [[INL]] they have experience doing this. 

I know I'm repeating myself, but this is why you have to get to the second-of-a-kind, third-of-a-kind, etc... so that it can be useful for the DoD.

The Naval Nuclear program isn't just taking a 1940s sub and putting in batteries, they completely redesigned the sub about what those capabilities were... so we need to be thinking bigger than just replacing diesel generator sets with nuclear reactors.

Please walk us through this! Nuclear is one of the most insanely dangerous things on the planet that hasn't hardened anyone, but that's because we make dangerous things safe. What's the plan?
 
A: Well, Pele 1.0 isn't going to be refueled at all! But it's typically driven by your transportation requirements... about how you're going to get that waste back, because typically waste in the US is only done by rail, and if you can only use these reactors where there are rail spurs, you're going to be limited... you want to make sure that you can transport waste over bridges, under tunnels, things like that.
- I do think smaller reactors will gain some efficiency by getting into Nth-of-kind... but there's n==o reason why microreactors will always be less efficient than bigger reactors, but will always be more expensive on a per-KWh basis than a big reactor==

In much of Alaska, they're paying >40c/KWh for Electricity. If you look island-specific, Hawaii is close to 40c/KWh, and some islands are worse than that.... so in some cases, even having a 40 cents/KWh microreactor... can still be sold to places that are willing 

I wouldn't power a datacenter with 1 reactor, because any reactor is going to have maintenance time, so 24/7/365 is dicey; being able to meet that with a few 100MW [[Small Modular Reactor|SMR]]s makes sense, but doing it with a bunch of [[Microreactor]] doesn't make sense in the contiguous 48 states.
