Q: What are the limitations around [[Uranium Dioxide]]?
A: It's boring and effective, so it's popular!  We manufacture by digging out of the ground, do some conversions for enrichment, and make a powder. To make the fuel, if you have a big press, you force it into a shape of a pellet, and if you have a big furnace, you can heat it up and get it down into the final size and shape you want... and basically that's it! You put in some rods, backfill with helium, and you're done. From a high level perspective, it's not super complicated to make; it's very robust, as as ceramic, it's not going to melt down.
- Very stable compared to other forms of [[Uranium]] like [[U308]]. If you want to operate in a reactor, the thermal conductivity there isn't as good, the shape isn't as good, the density isn't good.
- [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] operates a very happy medium space, where it's going to operate well for most of your applications in a Nuclear reactor, which is great. 
- It does have some downsides, though, which advanced fuels try to address!
	- It IS A CERAMIC. This provides advantages of very high melting points, but the counter to this is that it transfers heat quite poorly. 
		- If you're trying to get the heat out of the fuel pellet, it transfers slowly, which results, in terms of overall power output compared to a metallic fuel... the temperature of the fuel pellet itself is going to be much higher, especially in the center of the fuel pellet (since the heat is transferred on the outside). So you have to look at how close you are to the melting point. Luckily as a ceramic you have a high melting point, but it's not impossible.
			- We've seen UO2 pellets shrink in size over time... every time you increase the diameter, the ==centerline temperature== of these fuel pellets is going to increase.
	- It's prone to fracturing and breaking, because it's a ceramic. They're initially inspected for defects, chipping, voiding in the pellets, etc.. but as soon as you put these fuel pellets into the reactor and fire it up, the temperature goes up (slowly)... and those UO2 pellets continue densifying down (during the kiln firing and pressing during manufacturing to reduce volume and get as much uranium in the pellet as possible,, we only get to 95/98% theoretical density). In the reactor it compresses to 100%, so the fuel volume actually shrinks over the first month or so operating, meaning you then have a larger gap between the cladding and the fuel pellet itself, which eventually has to close.
		- As you start to acquire burnup, maybe with more rapid maneuvers in power, this can result in fracturing of the fuel pellet, so this nice matrix we had in place to capture fission gases and other nasty things coming out... is now a release path into the gap between the cladding and fuel pellet itself, so you have quite ready release of fission gas. 
		- If you look at XRay images of fuel pellets that have been in reactor, it's no long er a perfect pellet, it's full of jagged sections where the fracturing has occurred. It's not a problem generally, but it does mean that more fission gas escapes into the fuel pin, the void volumes of the fuel pin, which can increase pressure in the fuel pin.

In a nutshell, UO2 is great for being a ceramic, and also kind of sucky for being a ceramic, at the same time, but it's cheap, we know how to fabricate and do QC on it very well, and the issues are well-known, and we know how to operate to minimize some of the issues.

Q: Why can't we use convention fuel and cladding in a [[High Temperature Gas Reactor]]? 
A: Zirconium cladding won't melt at the temperatures we're talking about here, but if you have any oxygen in the environment, or hydrogen in the environment, zirconium doesn't hold up well. Also the centerline temperature of the pellets. In a [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]], the coolant (water in channels) is maybe 300C... and normal operating temperature of the centerline of the fuel pellet can be 1200C, simply because you have that poor of a heat transfer! These pellets produce kilowatts of thermal energy per foot, or multiple watts per centimeter... and that energy goes in both directions, towards the center and out, and it has to be dissipated. So if you're increasing from 300 to 800 in your coolant... heat transfer to a gas usually isn't as good, so it's more difficult to get it out, and if you add another 1000 or so degs to get to the fuel pin centerline temp, then you get up to 1800 degrees, and now that 2200 margin which looked great in a [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]] isn't looking as good in a [[High Temperature Gas Reactor|HTGR]].

Q: What is a ceramic again, and how is it not a metallic fuel if it's mostly uranium, a metal.
A: The most basic level of a ceramic... is some sort of oxygen-bound usually O2 or something... and that's about as far as I'm willing to remember/say on the chemistry side. When looking at a ceramic, it's usually a crystalling structure where you have grains that exist and grow, and it's repeating matrix grains that are able to then hold the other products in place when you have fission occurring. So if you have nice UO2 molecules all packed in  and oriented the same, at some point those uranium models are fissioned and displaced from high-speed fast Neutrons, same with the Oxygens in there... so you have a continuous rearrangement of the mechanical/chemical makeup inside. unlike a metallic fuel where atoms tend to rearrange themselves back into the original matrix, ceramics aren't as good at doing that, and so you have more displacement, deformation in the ceramic compared to other materials you'd see. UO2, being a more simple one, does experience more radiant.
Okay, so the oxygen part gives it its ceramic nature, got it.


Q: Let's talk about more advanced fuels... Let's talk about metallic, [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]], some of the narratives, costs.
- Let's start with [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]]...
	- In TRISO, the thing that maybe people don't fully recognize who just hear that it's great and meltdown proof... TRISO at its core is still [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]]! Tri-Isotopic Kernels, so very tiny poppyseed-sized kernels of [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] surrounded by a number of layers that provide additional structure and strenght. Some of the probelms of UO2 are solved, and some are not.
	- UO2 pellets are size of pink finger... now shrink down to the size of a poppy seed or smaller...
		- Now your heat transfer problem is much less; you don't have a full cm in diameter anymore that you have to get heat through...
		- But you DO still have the problem of hte fission gas buildup, and pellet swelling, and there still will be some cracking (not as bad as in UO2), still some internal cracking.
			- To solve this, when designing TRISO kernels, they have very small UO2 piece in the center, surrounded by a porous carbon buffer layer, whose entire purpose is to provide space for the kernel of the UO2 to expand, as it achieves more burnup, as the fission gasses build up. If this space wasn't there... you've seen pictures of a sidewalk where a tree is growing under, and eventually the tree pushes up the sidewalk, even though the sidewalk is cement, etc. eventually the pressure is too much. The carbon buffer layer provides this space.
			- Carbon as as buffer layer though isn't very hard as a material, so it would be susceptible to damage from the outside, and wouldn't contain any of the fission gasses (it's a porours layer, just there for expansion, it's like a carbon sponge). 
			- So we surround it with an inner pyrolytic carbon layer, which is just kind of a structural layer... on top of that, we put the silicone carbide layer. 
			- Silicone carbide is quite famous for being extremely robust, extremely difficult to break and puncture. This is the material that is credited with containing the fission gasses, preventing any kind of real mechanical damage (no fission particles can break out). Even if somehow you managed to get the UO2 inside to melt, [[Silicone Carbide]] has an incredibly high melting temperature... its' basically impossible for any of the UO2 material or fission products to get through this silicone carbide material.
			- Then we add another layer, the Tri part of the TRISO, which provides some structural stability to the whole thing, soo that if there's contact with other particles or other materials... this provides a nice bonding material for the silicone carbide, and a bound for the outside world outside of this little particle.
	- So these TRISO particles are quite nice, very hard to melt down, very small, etc.
	- This does mean that you need many controls on manufacturing them, and how they're used in the reactor, etc.

Q: So is moderation baked into that tristructural part, or are each of these poppy seeds embedded in a moderator? I understand there's Uranium metal because there's all this other stuff in TRISO fuel, I've heard 1/20th the amount of Uranium metal, so what does this mean in terms of the amount of levels of enrichment needed?
- In the TRISO particle itself, there's a small amount of Carbon that theoretically provides a small amount of moderation, but Carbon isn't effective compared to Water as a moderator, so individual TRISO particles are usually embedded in a carbon/graphite matrix (graphite is basically all carbon) which provides the moderation.
- The downside of this... is that you can really only pack in about 1/20th the amount of Uranium metal in any specific volume because of this, because you're using carbon as a moderator, which is much less effective compared to water, where you use hydrogen... you're packing in these very small particles and then surrounding most of the material in the TRISO particle itself... 
- And sod these two things combined: It's very difficult to pack in enough Uranium to make it work the same way that we have for [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]]s and [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] that's just sitting in a metal sleeve, and everything can just take place in a relatively compact space.
- Here, it becomes much less dense, the reactor core becomes much larger, and to counteract... if you don't want to make the core so ridiculously large... the other thing you have to do is crank the enrichment up (e.g. 4-5x higher), then you can shrink it back down to a reasonable size, but then the disadvantage is that you're using [[High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium|HALEU]], which is very expensive.

Q: How expensive? The cost of HALEU itself as this feedstock for [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]] and the triso manufacturing process...Also for ceramic fuels, there are production lines feeding hundreds of reactors around the world, operating all the time, there aren't gaps in production because there aren't enough reactors, etc... In terms of the projections of the HALEU part and TRISO part... how does that look in terms of cost, and how does it compare per-kg to ceramic [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]] fuel?
A: So [[High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium|HALEU]] is going to be more expensive
- Massive amount of Uranium to mine, and then essentially concentrating the [[U235]] atoms down into a smaller area, that's essentially what you do with enrichment.
- For 1kg of [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] at 5% enrichment, this costs ~$3000 for the Uranium itself
- For [[High-Assay Low-Enriched Uranium|HALEU]] at 20%, you're in for... ~$15000 for the Uranium itself. This is what arrives at the fuel fabrication factory.
- On top of that, for normal UO2, while fabrication is the most complex part... if you look at what actually goes on in these fabrication... in [[Light Water Reactor|LWR]]s, the cost of this is on the order of $300/kg, so it's not cheap, but it's not really that expensive anyways; the most expensive part is the Uranium inside of it, not the fabricated part that sits outside and holds it all together.
- In [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]] right now... the fabrication of TRISO is on the order of between $5000-$15000/kg, so add that to the $15,000 per kilogram for the HALEU itself... and this very expensive fabrication process... you're starting to shift a little bit away from "The uranium is the most expensive part" to now "the fabrication is expensive."
- Caveat: There are massive automated lines set up to do this... TRISO is not there yet, there isn't that type of heavy demand for it yet. A lot of it is made in very small batches by hand with individual engineers or technicians sitting there manipulating the equipment to get the exact mix/specifications they want. There's a pretty high reject rate at a lot of the steps... compared to UO2, where its' all automated... it shows the impact of automation and scale, once you get to that point.

Q: So maybe we'll get those fabrication costs... to my understanding, there isn't demand in the western world, we don't have a single TRISO fueled reactor operating in the western world. We have one, or maybe a pair in China (The [[Shidao Bay HTR-PM]], the world's first operational Generation IV nuclear power plant.)... A: In the US there are... ~100 reactors that are running, and there are 3 main fuel fabrication plants, which are generally at full capacity, though this doesn't mean that there's surging demand for their products.
- In TRISO, everything is at the lab, demonstration phase, not scaled up to these massive production lines. The process for producing - it is a bit more complicated than what we saw when talking about UO2, where you have a gas, convert it into a power, press into a pilot, densify it by firing it under heat, put it into a rod, and you're done.
- For TRISO, it's not so simple.
	- [[BWXT]] has a line that they're trying to get up and running, a demo line.
	- [[Standard Nuclear]] has another one.
	- [[X-Energy]] has their X-TRISO fabrication facility.
- ==They're all trying to not really get the cost down at the moment, but are focusing on being able to reliably and repeatably be able to manufacture a quality product.==
- For the UO2 lines, they do full-pellet inspections, gamma measurements, that all the welds are inspected, everything is there, etc.
- ==For TRISO, because you're producing these little tiny kernels, they're literally making billions of these kernels, and to get the QC down to the point where you're hopefully on par with what we're able to do with UO2.==
- Right now, the process for producing TRISO pellets (through it varies slightly company-to-company), they're:
	- Starting with [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]]
	- Grind it up and dissolve in acid to make a Uranium Nitrate, then try to precipitate it out and make tiny droplets in an aqueous solution.
	- Then they get them down and densify them in another chemical process...
	- And that's just to get little UO2 kernels out.
	- From images... they're supposed to be perfectly round spheres, but most spheres... half of the kernels coming out of the process aren't quite spheres, they're imperfect... If you're going to say that we have a known amount of UO2 in a kernel, and that we know the shape is going to be this... that needs to be what you're actually able to manufacture/produce! 
		- So there's a high reject rate of pellets... when they're producing these TRISO things... so you have to grind them back up to run them back through the process again, etc.
	- Then you have to get all these layers on them, where you suspend them in a gas and try to [[Powder Coat]] it from the outside, and have the same problem: Is it evenly distributed, same thickness? And doing multiple layers, etc.
		- You have to crush up rejects, try to debond materials, recover the expensive HALEU,.
	- ==So [[Tri-Structural Isotropic Fuel|TRISO]] is so expensive because you have such a difficult time in getting the quality of the product out compared to normal UO2 pellets.== 
		- If they have a reject with a UO2 pellet (eg a chip when fabrication), they grind it up and put it into the next batch of pellets coming out; they don't have to separate other materials from it, they aren't making billions of these and rejecting half of them, etc. The rejection of usual [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] pellets is well under 1%; the production is mature and reliable, whereas for TRISO it's not there yet.
		- They might get there if there is a market, e.g. 1000 reactors.
		- An appropriate analogy here... is if we looked at Transistors and microprocessors of the 50s, 60s, 70s, where you had a very small manufacturing process... and for a long time, it was very expensive. 
			- Personal computers in the 70s, 80s were very expensive, only large.
			- And now electronics are some of the cheapest things you can buy now.

Q: As I understand it, there are no reactors using [[Metallic Fuel]]... it's on pause. Let's talk about the history of metallic fuels.
A: First, a minor correction: In commercial space, there really are no metallic fuel plants that are running. The US Navy and maybe the Russian navy... runs a sort of metallic plate fuel, at least in older designs. In newer ones, they've moved towards a more matrix-type approach, but in general these are specific applications with really highly 90%-enriched fuel. And so that's probably not what we would be considering for power generation with someone like [[Oklo]] for instance.

A: But why do we want metallic fuel at all? IT comes back to some problems we discuss with [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]]
- UO2 has poor heat transfer, and good, but not great at retaining fission products, particularly fission gas.
- Metal, on the other hand, has fantastic heat conductance; you won't have these same centerline temperature problems, heat will come out much more quickly, so you can operate at a higher power level and not have the same centerline temperature as ceramic.

Soo... disadvantage of metallic is that:
- Melting temperature is lower compared to ceramic
- The heat transfer is much higher, which tends to offset the lower melting temperature

The other problem with [[Uranium Dioxide|UO2]] was the buildup of fission gas and when the fuel pellet cracks and grain boundaries separate, you get fission gas released into the plenum, the space between the fuel cladding and the fuel pallet. 
	In metallic fuel... because the metallic matrix tends to be very nice and if you have a displacement of a metal atom, they tend to rearrange themselves and put themselves back... the same is true when these new atoms are created... they tend to get "stuck" in this metal matrix, so your fission product gasses tend to be more well-contained; you don't have these big cracks that form because it's not a ceramic, and therefore there's a lot better containment of fission gasses so that they're not escaping into the plenum region or outside the fuel matrix itself.

Two great things from metallic:
- Better heat transfer
- Better containment of fission gasses
- ALSO: In UO2, you have Uranium and two oxygens, so the density of uranium you can get in there is much lower than what you can get in a metal-fueled reactor... so you can pack a whole lot more uranium in there.
Downside:
- Lower melting temperature; need better heat transfer; if yo lose that heat transfer, you're in a lot more trouble, a lot more quickly, because you have less of a margin before [[Nuclear Meltdown]].
