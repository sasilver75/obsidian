In a military/NATO medical doctrine, the medical continuum is broken down into progress Roles/Echelons of Care.

As a patient moves from Role 1 to Role 3, the medical capabilities expand from immediate, front-line life-saving first aid to advanced hospital surgery.


Role 1: Immediate and Tactical Care
- First medical care a service member receives, usually on battlefield or at point of injury.
- Tourniquets, airway management, etc. via combat medics, corpsmen, etc.
- Goal is to stabilize the casualty and prepare them for MEDEVAC to higher level of care.

Role 2: Advanced Resuscitation
- Serves as critical bridge before reaching a full hospital.
- Advanced trauma resuscitation, initial surgery (for damage control), blood transfusion via Medical officers, nurses, and surgical teams.
- Goal is to provide more extensive life/limb preservation and hold patients for a short period (up to 72 hours) before they're stable enough for further transport.

Role 3: Definitive Hospital Care
- The first level of comprehensive, definitive hospitalization, usually provided in a secure area in a large field hospital or hospital ship.
- Post-operative treatment, intensive care, advance surgical specialities, comprehensive imaging, etc.
- Goal is to deliver definitive surgical and medical care to all categories of patients.
