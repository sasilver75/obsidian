The ==process of maintaining a satellite in its intended orbit by periodically firing thrusters== to counteract perturbations that would otherwise cause it to drift.

Why orbits drift:
- A satellite in a "stable" orbit is actually constantly being nudged by:
	1. ==Earth's non-uniform gravity==; Earth's equatorial bulge ([[J2 Term]]) causes orbital [[Precession]].
	2. ==Lunar and Solar gravity==: Third-body perturbations, especially significant for [[Geostationary Orbit|GEO]]
	3. ==Atmospheric drag==: Significant in [[Low Earth Orbit|LEO]], negligible above ~800km
	4. ==Earth's triaxiality==: Earth is slightly elliptical at the equator, which causes GEO satellites to drift toward two stable "gravity wells" at ~75E and ~105W.

==Most critical for [[Geostationary Orbit|Geostationary]] satellites, which must stay within a tight box== (typically +/- 0.05 degrees) of their assigned longitude slot, in order to stay pointed at fixed ground antennas and not drift into adjacent satellites' slots (orbital slots are internationally allocated by the [[International Telecommunications Union]] (ITU)) 
- The ITU manages two scarce shared resources: The RF spectrum (so satellite transmitters get frequency allocations that don't interfere with others) and Orbital slots ([[Geostationary Orbit|GEO]] slots , longitude positions above the equator.)

Two components:
- North-south station keeping (correcting inclination drift caused by lunar/solar gravity, most propellant-expensive)
- East-west station keeping (corrects longitudinal drift caused by Earth's triaxiality)

When a satellite runs out of propellant for station keeping, it will drift uncontrollably; [[Geostationary Orbit|GEO]] satellites are typically boosted to a [[Graveyard Orbit]] (~300km above GEO) before propellant runs out, to free the slot.
