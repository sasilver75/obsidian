Joining materials, usually metals or thermoplastics, by fusing them together with (heat) fusion, pressure, or both. Some methods fully melt the base metal, while others join material in a solid or softened state. Filler material may be added to build the weld and help form a strong connection.


Core Concepts:
- ==Base Metal==: The parts being joined
- ==Filler metal==: Extra material added to help form the joint.
- ==Weld pool==: The molten metal created during many welding processes.
- ==Shielding==: Protection from oxygen/nitrogen in the air, usually by gas, flux, or vacuum.
- ==Heat-affected zone==: The area near the weld pool that did *not* melt, but had its properties changed by heat.
- ==Joint design==: The geometry o the connection, just as a (butt joint, lap joint, tee joint, corner joint, or edge joint)


==A good weld depends on matching the specific welding process to the material, thickness, strength requirement, environment, access, production volume, and inspection standard.==

Common welding processes:
- ==Arc Welding==: Uses an electric arc to generate heat and melt metal at the joint. Usually needs shielding gas or [[Flux]] to protect the holt weld from atmospheric contamination.
	- ==Stick Welding== / ==Shielded Metal Arc Welding (SMAW)==: Uses a consumable [[Flux]]-coated electrode; rugged, portable, common in construction, repair, pipelines, and field work.
	- ==MIG== / ==Gas Metal Arc Welding (GMAW)==: Uses a continuously fed wire electrode and shielding gas; fast, easy to learn, common in fabrication, automotive, and production welding.
	- ==TIG== / ==Gas Tungsten Arc Welding (GTAW)==: Uses a non-consumable tungsten electrode and separate filler if needed; slower by very precise, common for stainless steel, aluminum, aerospace, thin material, and high-quality welds.
	- ==Flux-cored Welding== / ==Flux-Cored Arc Welding (FCAW)==: Uses tubular wire filled with flux; good for heavy fabrication, outdoor work, and high deposition rates.
- ==Resistance Welding==: Joins metals using electrical resistance and pressure. Parts are pressed together, current flows through the contact area, and resistance creates heat at the joint. The metal softens/melts locally, and then solidifies under pressure.
	- ==Spot welding==: Makes small circular welds between overlapping sheets, widely used in automotive body panels and sheet metal assemblies.
- ==Gas Welding==: Uses a flame, usually from burning fuel gas with oxygen, to heat and melt the [[Workpiece]]. Older and slower than many arc processes, but still useful where electrical equipment is inconvenient.
	- ==Oxy-fuel Welding== / ==Oxy-Acetylene Welding==: Uses oxygen and acetylene to create a hot flame.
- ==Laser Welding==: Uses a concentrated laser beam to melt a narrow, precise joint. Fast, accurate, and produces a small heat-affected zone, making it useful for automotive, medical devices, electronics, batteries, aerospace, and precision manufacturing.
- ==Electronic Beam Welding==: Uses a focused beam of high-speed electrons, usually in a vacuum. Can make very deep, narrow welds with excellent precision, but the equipment is expensive and specialized.
- ==Friction Welding==: Joins parts using mechanical motion and pressure, instead of an open flame or arc. Parts are rubbed, rotated, or stirred against eachother until heat softens the material, then pressure forges the joint together. Strong and clean because it's often a solid-state process, meaning the material doesn't fully melt. 
	- ==Rotary Friction Welding==: One round part spins against another
	- ==Friction Stir Welding==: A rotating tool stirs material along a joint without fully melting it.
