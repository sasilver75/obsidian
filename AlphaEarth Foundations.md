Abstract:
> Unprecedented volumes of Earth observation data are continually collected around the world, but high-quality labels remain scarce given the effort required to make physical measurements and observations. This has led to considerable investment in bespoke modeling efforts translating sparse labels into maps. Here we introduce ==AlphaEarth Foundations==, an ==embedding field model yielding a highly general, geospatial representation== that ==assimilates spatial, temporal, and measurement contexts== across multiple sources, enabling accurate and efficient production of maps and monitoring systems from local to global scales. The embeddings generated by AlphaEarth Foundations are ==the only to consistently outperform a suite of other well-known/widely accepted featurization approaches tested on a diverse set of mapping evaluations without re-training==. We have released a ==dataset of global, annual, analysis-ready embedding field layers from 2017 through 2024==.

A geospatial foundation model from [[DeepMind|Google DeepMind]] that produces "embedding fields," dense 64-byte vectors at 10m square resolution covering Earth's land surfaced, released annually from 2017-2014 on [[Google Earth Engine]] (GEE).
- Not open-weights; you can't fine-tune the model itself, unlike something like [[Prithvi v2]]
- Produced the [[AlphaEarth Foundations|Google Satellite Embeddings]] dataset, a global analysis-ready collection of learned geospatial embeddings ([LINK](https://developers.google.com/earth-engine/datasets/catalog/GOOGLE_SATELLITE_EMBEDDING_V1_ANNUAL))

Key innovations:
- Multi-source fusion
	- Optical ([[Sentinel|Sentinel-2]], [[Landsat]])
	- Radar ([[Sentinel|Sentinel-1]], [[PALSAR|PALSAR-2]])
	- LiDAR ([[Global Ecosystem Dynamics Investigation|GEDI]])
	- Climate (ERA5, GRACE)
	- Elevation ([[Copernicus DEM|GLO-30]])
	- Annotations (NLCD, Wikipedia + GBIF species records)
- Continuous time modeling: First [[Remote Sensing|EO]] embedding approach to treat time as a continuous variable, not discrete snapshots. You can query embeddings for any date range, even interpolating/extrapolating beyond observed data.
- Space Time Precision (STP) encoder: Three simultaneous pathways at different resolutions:
	- 1/16 L spatial self-attention ([[Vision Transformer|ViT]]-style)
	- 1/8 L time-axial self-attention
	- 1/2 L convolutional precision path
- Interleaved with learned Laplacian pyramid exchanges to main both global context and local precision.
- Batch uniformity objective: Forces embeddings onto the unit sphere (S^63), preventing representational collapse.


Performance:
- ~23.9% error reduction over next-best method across 15 evaluations
- Outperforms [[Clay]], [[Prithvi]], [[SatCLIP]], and designed features ([[Continuous Change Detection and Classification|CCDC]], [[Multi-task Observation using Satellite Imagery & Kitchen Sinks|MOSAIKS]]) consistently across all tasks; the ==first approach where no single competitor wins anywhere==.
- Only 64 bytes per embedding vs. 16x more for next-most-compact earned method
- ~==480M parameter model== (smaller variant chosen for inference efficiency)

Our goal with AEF was to treat the set of acquisitions as a movie, from which they oculd extract a feature space, an embedding space, to characterize every pixel on the surface of the earth.

Fundamental assumptions about an embedding:
![[Pasted image 20260422200208.png]]
- Model was not necessarily for finetuning, but rather to directly output a useful embedding that flexibly accommodated/harmonized a bunch of different data sources and streams.
- Multiple modalities (images as a raster, as well as text) used to describe the surface of the earth.
- Needed embeddings to be spatially and temporally precise: Exactly over what period does an embedding apply? So they're a footprint on the ground but also a window of time.
- Embeddings need to be small; if you're a researcher that's using classical ML (many of them), you need a feature vector that you can actually manage in a regression or classification workflow.
	- They ended up with a 64-dimensional vector, which is very compact as far as embedding vectors go.
- Embeddings needed to be consistent; need them to be semantically meaningful, capturing real concepts... and minimize the ned for going on and doing more complicated modeling.
	- The goal is for people to get to answers faster by building on the embeddings! Making it easy for users!

That's quite a list of wants! So how did they do it:


![[Pasted image 20260422200509.png]]
can think of it as a classic [[Encoder-Decoder Architecture]].
- Images come in, are encoded into a latent representation space.
- We give the model a datetime, time code, and say: Hey model, take one of your decoder and decode into a different image source.
- Those targets can be anything from held-out images from input streams, or entirely different data sources, which then allows us to encode that information into the model weights.
- The point: The latent representation... the embedding we want, sits between the datastream coming in, and the task we decode on the output side.

Starting with the encoding
![[Pasted image 20260422200642.png]]
- Individually-timestamped images; ==they have precise timing, and we treat them like a video==, so we respect their sequencing coming in.
- Preprocessing blocks handle the primary image sources separately
- A couple steps where the sources are processed into features
- We then stack the giant feature vector together, together with their time codes (skinny green), and then pass them throguh these "STP Blocks" (Space-Time Precision), which is where most of the work goes on.
- Space is how we handle our spatial dependencies.
	- When the model is given an input image, it's able to attend to an entire image chip (1.28x1.28km on a side, so the model can account for long-distance relationships using the spatial pathway...)
- Time is handled in a similar way, except instead of attending tot the entire image chip, they can attend to other images in our time series.
	- We're able to look for things like seasonal phenomenon, like snow cover coming and going; the model can look back through the history of images and use that temporal context.
- Precision:
	- If space is the long term relationships, precision is how we get that very crisp local detail. They use a convolutional pathway where they use a tiny window over each 10m pixel in the output to not only understand long term relationships, but also the localized context.
- Model is able to cross-combine these spatial, temporal, and precision contexts...and all of the differentm easurment spaces it's being asked to translate betewen.

![[Pasted image 20260422201017.png]]
Another important part that goes on the backside of these STP blocks is our summarizer!
- We're out of our STP blocks, and now we're heading into a featurized versions where we've done all of the ross-combining of these feature dimensions and axes... and we still have our time codes, but we don't have a single summary, we have this long vector of output features.
- What we want .. like howwhen we make acomposite and set a compositing period, we want an embedding to represent a speciic period.
- We give the summarizer a "Valid period" over which we want it to summarize observations. Out of that, we get an embedding that summarizes a specific period, out of all of the observations that we've seen.
- This is how we get some of our temporal precision, as well as spatial precision.
- That yellow/red/blue square in the embedding; that's what we surface in our [[AlphaEarth Foundations|Google Satellite Embeddings]] dataset.

How do we learn to create a good embedding, though?
- Reconstruction is one of the main tasks that we use for learning.
- Here's a much more detailed view of our actual decoding process:
	- We're able to take the embedding vector, sample it using a [[von Mises-Fisher Distribution]] , where we're able to sample uncertainty on this 64-dimensional hypersphere that the embeddings live on... and we take those sampled embeddings with some uncertainties and use them to reconstruct different image targets!
	- On the right, they're seeing reconstructions of the [[Joint Emissivity Database Initiative]] (JEDI) dataset, which isn't included in the input sources, but we're ==asking the model to take the embedding and reproduce the JEDI image... and compare that to where we actually have JEDI measurements!==
	- Similarly, they've got a ==Snetinel-1 image==, so reproducing images that it hasn't seen in training
	- And finally some semantic informtaion, they ==used [[National Land Cover Database|NLCD]] a==s a weak constriant on reconstruction to make sure that there's some semantic meaning... in that embedding space.
But it's not just those ==three== sources!

![[Pasted image 20260422202920.png]]
Being able to reconstruct targets as the objective...
But there were three other objectives at play:
1. ==Batch Uniformity objective==: Used to pair a shifted version of our input data batch.... we take an element from a sample (a video clip) and pair wt with a different sample, and optimize that the embedding is learn to minimize the dot product between the two embeddings. We want the embeddings tobe perpendicular to eachotehr for different batch elements. Has nice result that they're well-distributed over this unit sphere (S^63, since 64-dim). Gives us this uniform distribution and normal range.
2. Contrastive Consistency: Makes sure that we're robust to the reality of working with remote sensing data. Sensors might drop out, we might have missing inputs, gaps in coverage, etc. It's a Student-Teacher model setup where the student is given a subset of possible images, and is encourage to create an embedding that looks like that by a teacher that has full information... lets us make sure that if we lose Sentinel-1 (which is the reality of S1 coverage for the last year or so), we're still able to generate a full, consistent embedding even if we don't have one of our core inputs.
3. Text contrastive objective: Similar tot he student teacher with contrastive consistnetcy... but her we align our EO embeddigns iwth the embeddings that are frozen outo ht e text encoders (eg. tehte article ocntext). We're saying: Take that EO embedding and make sure that it's aligned with teh tetext embedding, so that we're albeo t pull in some of the semantic informatino there.

It's the combination of all of these objectives thatl et us buidl a featuer spce that's really sueful for the things we want to use it for!
