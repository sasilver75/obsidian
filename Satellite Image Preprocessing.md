# [[Geospatial Data Abstraction Library|GDAL]]/ [[Geospatial Data Abstraction Library|OGR]]
- The single most important software library ever created for geospatial.
- Maintained by [[Open Source Geospatial Foundation]] (OSGeo), which is just a consortium of people who want good software!
- Split into two components, mostly for historical reasons (there used to be two libraries)
	- GDAL: Geographic Data Abstraction Library ~~ For Raster things
	- OGR: OpenGIS Simple Features Reference Implementation ~~ For Vector things
- They both basically do the same things: 
	- Resampling
	- Reprojection
	- Clipping
	- etc.
- Eventually the OGR group fell apart, it wasn't being maintained as well, and eventually it was sort of merged into the GDAL library. So you'll see people either refer to BOTH things as GDAL, or refer to GDAL and OGR separately. It doesn't really matter.

Python wrappers:
- [[rasterio]]: Hand-crafted Python bindings for GDAL (==Raster==)
- [[Fiona]]: Hand-crafted Python bindings for OGR (==Vector==)
Note: GDAL also released their own python bindings (autogenerated), called `osgeo`, but... you can tell it's autogenerated, it's not great, and has strange quirks and not great documentation (they assume you'll read the C++)

## Coordinate Reference Systems
- The earth is a 3D sphere, but our images are 2D.
- When we take an image of the Earth, we lose some of that 3D curvature.
- We need some sort of ==projection== to map the 3d surface onto a 2d image, and need a coordinate reference system to refer back to the 3D structure, so that we know where it's located on the sphere.
- A common one is [[Mercator]].
	- This is known as an ==Equal Angle, but not Equal Area== projection.
		- The shape is correct, but the areas are completely wrong.
		- In reality, Africa is 15x the size of Greenland!
- You could also use something like [[Albers Equal Area]], which is ==Equal Area, but not Equal Angle== projection.
	- All of the shapes look weird, distorted, and squished, but the areas are correct; these are the actual relative sizes.
- If you have data in two different projections, they're not going to line up! You need all of your data to be in the same CRS.
- There's ==literally thousands of other projections that will minimize distortion over landmases, or over ocean, or over just one country you're studying at the time, etc.==
- ![[Pasted image 20260423005211.png]]
- There's not one correct coordinate system, they all have tradeoffs.


## Resolution and TAP
- Let's say you download [[Sentinel|Sentinel-2]] and another dataset as well, and notice that S2 has some bands at 10m/pix, others at 20m/pix, etc. 
	- Pixels are different sizes
- You can't easily combine these datasets without resampling them to a  common resolution!
- Or you could have two images that are in exactly same resolution... but this doesn't mean they are ML ready. The second image could be shifted by half a pixel! We need to shift it!

- You need to make sure you're
	- In same coordinate reference system
	- Have same resolution
	- Are grid-aligned
		- In GDAL, this is known as ==Target-Aligned Pixels, or TAP==; it refers to making sure everything is on the same grid, not shifted.

If you have a camera in your pocket, that camera is not measureing color, it's measuring teh number of photons of a certain wavelength that hit a sensor.
- That's really just some sort of random number that dependso n how long your camera aperture is open for and some other factors.
- We need some physical units to represent our colors!
	- This process is known as [[Radiometric Calibration]]: Using physical units to calibrate our sensor... so that regardless of how old the camera is, or how long the shutter is open for, it still has the same meaning.
- You don't need to know this formula
	- The goal is to have the amount of light radiated by the earth, specifically at the top of the atmosphere... our goal is to get physical units for much light is hitting the satellite...
	- This depends on a few things, like angle of sun, angle of satellite, ...
	- Usually you'll calibrate the sensors on the ground in the factory
		- Here's a reference image, we know its exact color, etc. We can calibrate this camera
	- In space it still might start to drift, so you'll look at specific large reference objects on the ground and recalibrate in space, as our sensors get old.
- ==Gets us from some raw digital number (eg number of photons, whatever) to an actual usable measure of light with units.==


![[Pasted image 20260423010450.png]]
- Geometric Correction
	- You kind of know where the satellite is, and you kind of know where the satellite is pointing (usually somewhat straight down, sometimes a little off to the side),  If you do some math, you kind of know where the satellite is
	- Depending on how good your telemetry is... +/- 1km for old satellites, newer satellites +/- 10 meters or 100meters
		- So we're much better at it than we used to be
	- But if we're doing time series analysis where we have an image of the same place, taken every two weeks for the entire year...
		- Every pixel needs to be lined up perfectly with every other pixel! We don't want buildings moving around, etc!
- We can use known reference points, [[Ground Control Point]]s, to morph/distort our image onto the right shape.


![[Pasted image 20260423010708.png]]
- At any given time, the Earth's surface is covered 75% or so in clouds.
- It may be partially cloudy and half the image is useful, and the other half blows up your ML model and it doesn't know what to do.
- So cloud removal is really important!
	- We really usually mean [[Cloud Masking]], so we know which pixels are useful and trustworthy, and which ones aren't.
	- But it could also be actually trying to "fill in the gaps" and replace it with the image before and after, or something like that.
- There are different types of clouds: There are some thin, ==whispy== areas and there are thick ==white== clouds, with different levels of opacity. Clouds also have a ==shadow==.
- The goal is to get a good cloud mask that tells us which pixels are trustworthy and which are not-so-trustworthy.

We would like to convert this [[Top-of-Atmosphere]] radiance to what we expect the actual [[Surface Reflectance]] is.
- We do this in a way that's based on physics.
- For different molecules, we can tell what scattering effects they might have. There are things like Co2 or Water Vapor that havea known pattern of scattering as a result.
- You can do studies and figure out how to translate between radiance and top-of-atmosphere... and how much of the light is a result of scattering, and how much is true light from the surface.
- See going from the bottom image to the top one.
- None of the steps work perfectly, but the people who do this science are really smart and have done a lot of hard work for us.

	- So if we take an image, and we have an elevation mdoel, and we know which direction the satellite was pointing, we can correctly deal with topographic distortion!

Depending on the angle of the sun and the angle of the satellite, the surface of the earth looks quite different.
- [[Bidirectional Reflectance Distribution Function]] (BDRF) lets us correct for differences in angle of sun and sensor.
These picture pairs are the exact same place at the exact same time, the only different is taking a picture facing the sun, or away from the sun, and the image you get as a result is completely different because of the angle the ground is getting hit by the sun.

We really want our images to look comparable, regardless of what time of day they were taken, or what time of year they were taken.

# Proprocessing :[[Hyperspectral]] image processing

It's more or less the same as for [[Multispectral|MSI]]... PLUS some extra steps..

Recall: Why HSI? Instead of focusing on a few narrow bands that we know are useful for certain applications (e.g. NIR for ag), with HYperspectral, we capture every wavelength, and capture very very fine, almost continuous distributions of wavelengths across teh specturm.

The goal is to separate very similar looking things by their spectral signature!
- This is almost like a fingerprint, or a mass spectrometer flying around the earth, taking images.
- You should be able to measure very minute changes in things.
- You should be able to see differences in the chemical composition of the soil, for instance.
- Some people looking at forests in Switzerland, for instance... they're trying to tell what species of trees soemthing is, and saying that there are diffretn varieties of species, can we tell the difference between these different varieties?

For hyperpsectral imaging, we can basically do the same steps we did for multspectral:
1. [[Denoising]]
2. [[Pansharpening]]
3. [[Dimensionality Reduction]]
4. [[Spectral Unmixing]]
5. [[Spectral Matching]]

Denoising
- MSI and HSI often have a high signal-to-noise ratio
- Because we're trying to capture a very narrow wavelength of the EM spectrum, the sensor doesn't allow a lot of photons IN
	- As a result, you get... 6 photons plus or minus five!
	- You get a lot of noise as a result of this narrow bandwidth, and you need to be able to remove that noise.
- There's other sources of noise
	- Thermal (Johnson) Noise, Quantization Noise, ...
- You can zoom out and capture more light, but then you lose resolution
- How do we do denoising?
	- The most common approach is a [[[Non-Local Meets Global]] algorithm, which is an iterative algorithm.
	- ![[Pasted image 20260423012847.png]]
	- You keep calculating until it stabilizes.




![[Pasted image 20260423013120.png]]
[[Pansharpening]]
- MSI has high spatial resolution, but low spectral resolution, seeing a highly-resolved image, but you only see RGB
- If you want to get more spectral resolution like a HSI, you have to sacrifice photons; low spatial resolution, high spectral.
- Different spectral bands sometimes have different resolution
	- Landsat: Almost all bands have 30m/pix, but the wide [[Panchromatic]] band has 15m resolution!
- Can we do [[Data Fusion]] and combine them to get the best of both worlds?
	- Getting super-resolved hyperspectral imagery that's actually based on physics and science, and not guessing
		- You can also do this with ML, which is 
	- This instead is based on known high resolution and high spectral resolution information
	- You use something called [[Coupled Non-Negative Matrix Factorization]]


![[Pasted image 20260423014338.png]]
Another problem unique to hyperpspectral data is that we have too much data!
- Maybe instead of Multispectral RGB with 3 colors, we now have 250 different colors!
- This is a big data problem; your computer might not be abble to fit this in memory, your ML model might struggle.
- We'll often times only use the parts that are relevant to us, from the data wecollect.
- If somethign is bright red, it's probably also (bright red+1/2 nanometer); there's a lot of correlation between nearby spectral bands, which means we don't need all the information. 
	- The problem is we don't know which information we DO need!
- There are lots of ways to reduce dimensionality:
	- If you can figure out what bands you need, just use those!
	- Otherwie:
		- [[Singular Value Decomposition]] (SVD)
		- [[Principal Component Analysis]] (PCA)
		- [[Independent Component Analysis]] (ICA)
		- NMF (Non-Negative Matrix Factorizastion
		- MNF: Maximum Noise Function

Spectral Unmixing
- For low-res imagery where you still want to be able to separate out pixels, and zoom in... you can do [[Spectral Unmixing]]
- In the image on the right, we have two materials, a blue thing on the earth, and a yellow thing on the earth.
	- All of those green pixels you see... part of the tpixel is the blue thing, part is the yellow thing; the result is a blurry green blog.
	- Can we figure out what's in that green pixel -- is it half blue and half yellow? Is there another color mixed in that we don't know about?
- What we really want is ==pixel-wise probability for the different "endmembers" for every pixel in our image!==
- We decompose the spectral signature of these pixels with multiple colors into their constituent components.
- How? There's a pixel purity index you can use where you look for "extrema" (unique, simplified color spectra)... and then all the other colors you see are superpositions of two different color spectrum.
	- A similar idea to [[Fourier Series]]

1. Create a database of reference hyperspetral signatures of various materials (often in a lab) (collect leaves from species of trees, photograph them with hyperspectral sensor under similar conditions)
2. Identify each endmember class by comparing its spectra against one or more reference spectra.
